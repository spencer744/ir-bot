# Fairmont Apartments — FAQ: Risks, Downside Scenarios & Gray Capital's Position

## Primary Risks

---

### Risk 1: Acquisition Basis / Price Premium

**The Concern:** $285K/door is a 17–78% premium to recent Columbus Class A sale comps. The highest comparable trade (The Meridian) closed at $245K/door. Are we overpaying?

**Our Position:** The premium is justified by what you're buying — and disappears when you adjust for the abatement.

Fairmont carries a 15-year, 100% property tax abatement generating ~$895K/year in tax savings with a $12.2M NPV over the remaining term. No comparable asset carries this. When you adjust for the abatement's NPV, the effective basis drops to **~$230K/door** — in-line with recent trades for older, less-amenitized product.

Additionally, Fairmont is 2025 construction by Columbus's premier developer. The Meridian, Allure at Polaris, and other comps are 2018–2022 vintage. You're comparing a flagship 2025 product with best-in-market amenities to older Class A stock. Replacement cost today is $350K+/door — we're buying 15–20% below that.

**The effective yield tells the real story:** Including the abatement, Fairmont's effective yield on total capital is 6.18% — 75–89 bps above where The Meridian (4.95%) and Allure at Polaris (5.10%) traded for inferior, unabated product. We're paying more, but getting significantly more.

### Risk 2: Tax Abatement Dependency

**The Concern:** Without the abatement, LP IRR collapses from base case to ~8.0% (an 870 bps reduction). One year of CRA non-compliance triggers permanent rescission through 2041.

**Our Position:** The risk is administrative, not economic. The 66 AMI units are already leased at 80% AMI rents — we're not restructuring anything. Gray Capital will implement dedicated compliance infrastructure from Day 1:
- Annual income certification on all 66 restricted units
- Annual CRA compliance reporting filed **90 days before** the March 31 deadline
- Ohio-qualified property manager with abatement compliance experience
- Abatement transferability confirmed with notice to municipality

Gray Capital staff have extensive experience in affordable housing management and compliance. We've navigated AMI compliance programs before. This is operational discipline, which is within our control.

The deal is still underwritten to deliver above the 8% preferred return even in the abatement-loss scenario — but we have no intention of being in that scenario.

### Risk 3: Metro Vacancy at 20-Year High

**The Concern:** Columbus vacancy hit 9.8% — a 20-year high. Class A rents are -0.3% YoY. The competitive set is offering 4–8 weeks free. Is the market in structural decline?

**Our Position:** 9.8% is a lagging indicator of the 2025 supply wave, not a structural problem. Columbus delivered 9,000+ new units in 2025 — an abnormal surge. Deliveries drop 43%+ in 2026–27; Westerville specifically drops 75%.

Columbus absorption is running **356% above prior year levels**. The market is digesting supply aggressively. We close as the supply cliff begins and Anduril Arsenal-1 starts production (4,008 jobs, July 2026).

Columbus is also the only major U.S. market to sustain zero negative lease trade-out months post-COVID while absorbing heavy supply — demonstrating structural demand depth that no other market has matched. The 9.8% vacancy is temporary. The employer base is permanent and growing.

---

### Risk 4: Rent Growth Assumption (4% Blended)

**The Concern:** Columbus Class A rents are -0.3% YoY. A 4% blended growth assumption appears aggressive.

**Our Position:** Our 4% is not a market appreciation bet. It's three verifiable engines embedded in the current rent roll:

1. **AMI escalator:** 5.3% CAGR on 66 units — built into the HUD structure, not dependent on market conditions
2. **Loss-to-lease capture:** ~$290K/year of existing mispricing corrects to market as leases expire. This is arithmetic, not speculation.
3. **3BR mark-to-market:** 23 units currently 5–8% below submarket average ($2,270). Natural correction on renewal.

**Stress test:** At flat 3% organic growth with a 5.50% exit cap, LP IRR is approximately 14.2%. Under the conservative scenario (3% growth, 6.00% exit cap), LP IRR is approximately 10.5% with a 1.8x equity multiple. We're not relying on a market rebound.

### Risk 5: Debt Service Coverage

**The Concern:** Is there adequate debt service coverage?

**Our Position:** Year 1 pro forma DSCR is **1.33x pre-abatement** ($3.0M NOI / $2.26M debt service) and **1.72x with abatement** ($3.9M / $2.26M). By Year 2, coverage with abatement grows to ~1.80x as NOI increases.

The 48-month interest-only period provides maximum cash flow during the critical early years. The $875K rate buydown reduces the stated rate and improves near-term cash flow. Fannie Mae agency debt eliminates refinancing risk during the hold — no floating rate exposure, no bridge loan maturity event.

Additionally, the $895K tax abatement functions as a cash flow backstop — even at flat revenue, the abatement alone represents ~40% of debt service coverage.

### Risk 6: 46% Lease Rollover by August 2026

**The Concern:** Nearly half the rent roll expires within 90 days of close. If Gray Capital can't execute renewals at target rents, occupancy and revenue could drop sharply.

**Our Position:** This is our primary operational risk and we're treating it accordingly:
- Gray Residential is **already coordinating with the seller** pre-close to ensure renewals are managed 90 days before expiration
- Gray Capital is reaching out to corporate employers (Anduril, Intel, Victoria's Secret, Bath & Body Works) in advance to identify preferred employer and corporate housing opportunities
- The 66 AMI units (near-zero turnover) are a stabilizing base within the 46% rollover figure
- Columbus absorption running 356% above prior year — market is absorbing supply, not rejecting it
- Westerville supply drops 75% in 2026 — fewer alternatives for renewing residents

The rollover is actually an opportunity embedded in the risk: 46% of leases rolling by August 2026 means 46% of the loss-to-lease capture happens within our first 6 months. We're moving fast by design.

### Risk 7: Entry Cap / Cap Rate Sensitivity

**The Concern:** 25 bps of exit cap rate widening from the 5.50% base reduces sale proceeds by ~$3.5M. The 140 bps spread to the 10-Year Treasury (4.46%) is thin.

**Our Position:** 5.50% is already conservative relative to where comps traded:
- The Meridian: 4.95% cap (55 bps tighter than our exit)
- Allure at Polaris: 5.10% cap (40 bps tighter than our exit)

We're exiting 7 years after those trades, with:
- ~8 years of abatement remaining (representing $7–8M NPV to next buyer)
- Intel Ohio One operational or approaching operation (~3,000 engineers at $135K+)
- Multi-year proven submarket outperformance

At a 6.00% exit cap (50 bps of widening), LP IRR is approximately 11.8%. That scenario would require simultaneously: higher rates, no abatement premium, and no Intel demand premium.

### Risk 8: Intel Ohio One Timeline Risk

**The Concern:** Intel's Ohio semiconductor campus is referenced as a demand catalyst, but Intel has faced financial headwinds, workforce reductions, and timeline adjustments to its U.S. fab expansion plans.

**Our Position:** Intel Ohio One is presented as unmodeled upside — it is not in our base case underwriting. The deal's projected returns assume existing demand only. If Intel delivers on its planned timeline (2030–31), it would be a significant demand catalyst with ~3,000 engineers at $135K+ average salary and 10,000+ secondary jobs. If it is delayed or reduced, our base case is unaffected.

That said, the broader Columbus advanced manufacturing and technology corridor is real and diversified beyond Intel alone. Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and Meta are expanding data center operations in northeast Columbus. Anduril's $1.8B defense campus is independently progressing. The submarket's demand base does not depend on any single employer.

Investors should be aware that Intel's timeline is subject to change based on corporate priorities, CHIPS Act funding allocations, and global semiconductor market conditions.
