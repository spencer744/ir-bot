## Supply & Demand Dynamics

Columbus absorbed 9,000+ new units in the 2025 supply wave — which explains the temporary 9.8% metro vacancy rate (a 20-year high) and current concession environment. **This is a lagging indicator, not a structural problem.**

**The supply cliff is underway:**
- Columbus MSA new deliveries drop 43%+ in 2026–27
- Westerville/New Albany submarket deliveries drop **75%** in 2026 specifically
- Columbus absorption is running **356% above prior year** levels as the supply wave is digested

**The setup:** We're acquiring as the supply cliff begins and Anduril Arsenal-1 starts production. The market is already absorbing faster than it's delivering new units. Pricing power is shifting back to landlords.

**New Albany** is ranked #25 Best City to Live in the U.S. (USA Today, 2025) and recognized by Business Insider as one of America's premier suburbs. Planning commission has historically limited multifamily approvals, and available infill land is essentially exhausted — creating a structural supply constraint that supports durable occupancy and rent growth.

**Westerville City School District:** 14,250 students, 12th largest district in Ohio. Rated "Excellent with Distinction" for 16 consecutive years. Named a "What Parents Want" designation by SchoolMatch. Three high schools (Central, North, South). Top-performing schools are a primary retention anchor for family renters choosing Class A product at $1,785/mo over homeownership (Source: Westerville City Schools, SchoolMatch).

**Rent-to-income ratio: ~17–18%.** With a 3-mile median HHI of ~$102K (ACS 2020–2024) and Fairmont's avg rent of $1,785/mo ($21,420/yr), rent-to-income sits well below the 30% threshold — indicating strong affordability and low default risk for a high-income, renter-by-choice demographic.

**Rent-vs-own gap:** Avg monthly cost of homeownership in Westerville is ~$4,831. Fairmont's average rent is $1,785. The 63% cost advantage anchors a high-income, renter-by-choice demand base with very low price sensitivity.

**Barriers to entry:**
- Restrictive municipal zoning (New Albany and Westerville)
- Scarce available land for new multifamily
- High development costs with long entitlement timelines
- No meaningful competitive deliveries expected 2026–2027

## Rent Growth Context

Columbus Class A rents are currently -0.3% YoY — a temporary condition driven by the 2025 supply peak. Our 4% rent growth assumption is not a market appreciation bet:

1. **AMI escalator:** 5.3% CAGR on 66 units (built into the abatement structure)
2. **Loss-to-lease capture:** ~$290K of existing mispricing corrects to market on natural rollover
3. **3BR mark-to-market:** 5–8% below submarket average with natural correction on renewal

**Stress test:** At flat 3% organic growth, LP IRR still clears 12.5%. The deal does not depend on a market rebound.

## Columbus Market Resilience

Columbus ranks #1 in the U.S. for rent resilience: no negative lease trade-out months post-COVID, sustained through one of the heaviest new supply cycles of any Midwest market. Peer markets achieving the same result (no negative months) did so through supply restriction alone — Columbus achieved it with consistent new supply, proving the depth of underlying demand.
