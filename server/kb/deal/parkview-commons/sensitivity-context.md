# Parkview Commons — Sensitivity Analysis Context

## Purpose of Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity analysis stress-tests the base case underwriting by varying critical inputs to illustrate the range of possible outcomes for Parkview Commons. Real estate investments involve uncertainty, and understanding how changes in key assumptions affect projected returns helps investors evaluate risk and make informed decisions. This document explains what drives returns up and down, how to interpret sensitivity tables, and where the breakeven thresholds lie.

## Base Case Assumptions

The base case projections are built on the following key assumptions:

| Assumption | Base Case Value |
|-----------|----------------|
| Purchase Price | $42,000,000 |
| Total Equity | $15,000,000 |
| Annual Rent Growth | 3.5% |
| Stabilized Occupancy | 94% |
| Exit Cap Rate | 5.25% |
| Interest Rate | 5.50% fixed |
| Renovation Cost per Unit | $15,000 |
| Hold Period | 5 years |
| Annual Debt Service | $2,020,000 |

**Base Case Returns:** 15.8% IRR | 1.95x Equity Multiple | 7.2% Avg Cash-on-Cash

## What Drives Returns Up

Several factors can push returns above the base case projection:

### Stronger Rent Growth
If annual rent growth exceeds the 3.5% base case assumption, both operating cash flow and the eventual sale price increase. Indianapolis has experienced 3.5% year-over-year rent growth recently, and the upside scenario models 4.5% growth. Each additional 1% of annual rent growth compounds over five years, increasing cumulative cash flow and exit NOI. At 4.5% annual growth, the IRR moves into the 19-21% range.

### Higher Occupancy
The base case assumes 94% stabilized occupancy, which is conservative compared to renovated comparables in the submarket (Meridian at Westfield at 95.2%, Brookside Crossing at 96.1%). If the renovation program achieves occupancy closer to 96-97%, annual NOI increases by roughly $100,000 to $150,000, boosting both cash flow and the capitalized exit value.

### Cap Rate Compression at Exit
If the exit cap rate is lower than the 5.25% base case, the sale price increases meaningfully. A 4.80% exit cap (the upside scenario) yields a substantially higher sale price, as NOI is divided by a smaller number. Each 25 basis point decrease in exit cap rate adds approximately $1-2 million to the sale price.

### Renovation Outperformance
If the renovation program achieves rent premiums above the projected $172 per month average, or if renovation costs come in under the $15,000 per unit budget, returns improve. The rent comparable data supports premiums up to $200-$225 per month for top-tier renovations.

## What Drives Returns Down

### Lower Rent Growth
If annual rent growth falls below 3.5%, both cash flow and exit value decline. At 2.0% growth (the conservative scenario), the IRR drops to approximately 11-12%. At near-zero growth, returns would approach or fall below the 8% preferred return threshold.

### Cap Rate Expansion at Exit
The exit cap rate is the single largest driver of total returns in value-add deals. If cap rates expand (increase) due to rising interest rates, market softening, or reduced buyer demand, the sale price drops significantly. A 5.75% exit cap (conservative scenario) reduces total returns meaningfully compared to the 5.25% base case. Each 25 basis point increase in exit cap rate reduces the sale price by approximately $1-2 million.

### Lower Occupancy
If the property struggles to maintain 94% occupancy due to increased competition, economic softness, or renovation disruption, cash flow declines. The conservative scenario models 91% stabilized occupancy. Each 1% decline in occupancy reduces annual NOI by approximately $50,000-$60,000.

### Renovation Cost Overruns or Delays
If interior renovations cost more than $15,000 per unit or exterior improvements exceed $780,000, additional equity may be required or returns will be diluted. Construction delays can extend the renovation timeline beyond 24 months, pushing the stabilization date further out and reducing time at full rental income before exit.

### Rising Operating Expenses
Insurance, property taxes, and utility costs have trended upward nationally. If expense growth materially exceeds underwriting assumptions, NOI margin compression reduces both cash flow and exit value.

## Breakeven Analysis

Breakeven analysis identifies the point at which the investment generates just enough income to cover obligations but produces no excess return. These thresholds represent the margin of safety built into the underwriting.

### Breakeven Occupancy: ~82%

The breakeven occupancy of approximately 82% is the level at which gross rental income covers total operating expenses plus annual debt service of $2.02 million. At this occupancy, there is no cash flow available for investor distributions, but debt obligations are met and the property avoids default.

**Context:** The base case assumes 94% stabilized occupancy, providing a 12-percentage-point cushion above breakeven. Even the conservative scenario at 91% occupancy remains 9 points above breakeven. The property's current occupancy of 94% and the submarket vacancy rate of 4.8% suggest that occupancy at or below 82% would require severe and sustained market distress.

### Breakeven Rent Growth: ~0.5% for IRR Below Preferred Return

If annual rent growth averages approximately 0.5% or less over the hold period (holding all other assumptions constant), the investment's IRR would fall below the 8% preferred return. This means investors would still receive a return of capital and some profit, but the IRR would underperform the preferred return threshold.

**Context:** Indianapolis has delivered 3.5% year-over-year rent growth, and the long-term historical average for the MSA is approximately 2.5-3.0%. Sustained rent growth below 0.5% would require a significant and prolonged economic downturn or a major supply shock in the submarket.

## How to Read Sensitivity Tables

Sensitivity tables (also called data tables) in the offering materials show how a specific return metric (typically IRR or equity multiple) changes as one or two input assumptions vary. Here is how to interpret them:

### Single-Variable Sensitivity
A single-variable table varies one assumption (for example, exit cap rate) while holding all other assumptions at their base case values. Read across the row to see how the return metric changes as the assumption moves from favorable to unfavorable.

### Two-Variable Sensitivity
A two-variable table (matrix) varies two assumptions simultaneously. The row headers show one variable (for example, rent growth) and the column headers show another (for example, exit cap rate). The cell at the intersection shows the resulting return metric. The base case is typically highlighted or centered in the table.

### Key Principles for Reading Sensitivity Tables

1. **Look at the base case first** to anchor your expectations, then examine how returns change as assumptions move in either direction.
2. **Pay attention to asymmetry.** Returns typically fall faster when assumptions worsen than they rise when assumptions improve. This is because leverage amplifies both gains and losses.
3. **Focus on the downside.** Upside scenarios are appealing but the most important question is: what happens if things go wrong? How much cushion exists before capital is at risk?
4. **Consider probability.** Not all scenarios are equally likely. A 5.75% exit cap rate in a rising-rate environment may be more probable than a 4.50% exit cap rate, for example.

## Historical Context

Understanding the historical range of key assumptions provides perspective on the reasonableness of the base case:

- **Indianapolis Rent Growth:** Has averaged approximately 2.5-3.5% annually over the past decade, with recent years at the higher end. The 3.5% base case assumption is in line with recent performance.
- **Multifamily Cap Rates (Indianapolis):** Class B cap rates in Indianapolis have ranged from approximately 4.75% to 6.25% over the past decade, depending on asset quality, location, and interest rate environment. The 5.25% base case exit cap represents a middle-of-the-range assumption.
- **Occupancy:** Indianapolis MSA vacancy has been between 4% and 7% over the past decade. The current 4.8% vacancy rate is healthy. The 94% base case occupancy (6% vacancy) assumption is conservative relative to current conditions.
- **Interest Rates:** The 5.50% fixed rate was locked at acquisition and does not change during the hold period. This eliminates interest rate risk on the debt during the investment.
