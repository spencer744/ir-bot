# Parkview Commons — Cost Segregation and Tax Estimates

## Cost Segregation Overview

Gray Capital will engage a qualified engineering firm to perform a **cost segregation study** on Parkview Commons shortly after acquisition. Cost segregation is an IRS-approved tax strategy that reclassifies building components from the standard 27.5-year residential depreciation schedule into shorter recovery periods of 5 and 15 years. This acceleration of depreciation deductions generates significant paper losses in the early years of ownership, which can offset passive income on investors' tax returns.

### How Cost Segregation Works

Standard depreciation for a residential rental property spreads the building's depreciable basis evenly over 27.5 years. A cost segregation study identifies specific components that qualify for shorter recovery periods:

| Asset Classification | Recovery Period | Examples |
|---------------------|----------------|---------|
| Personal Property | 5 years | Carpeting, appliances, window treatments, cabinetry, certain fixtures |
| Land Improvements | 15 years | Parking lots, landscaping, sidewalks, fencing, signage, site lighting |
| Building Components | 27.5 years | Structural elements, roofing, HVAC systems, plumbing, electrical |
| Land | Non-depreciable | The land itself is never depreciated |

By reclassifying a substantial portion of the building's value into 5-year and 15-year categories, investors can claim significantly larger depreciation deductions in the early years, reducing or eliminating taxable income from the investment even while receiving cash distributions.

## Bonus Depreciation

Under current federal tax law, **bonus depreciation** allows investors to deduct a percentage of qualifying short-lived assets (5-year and 15-year property) in the year they are placed in service, rather than depreciating them over their full recovery period.

For Parkview Commons, the applicable bonus depreciation rate is **40%**, which applies to the accelerated portion of the depreciable basis. This means that 40% of the approximately $21 million in 5-year and 15-year property can be deducted in Year 1, with the remaining 60% depreciated over the normal 5- and 15-year schedules.

## Depreciation Recapture at Sale

When the property is sold at the end of the five-year hold period, the IRS requires recapture of depreciation previously claimed. This is an important consideration when evaluating the net tax benefit of cost segregation:

- **Section 1245 Recapture:** Depreciation on personal property (5-year assets) is recaptured as ordinary income at the investor's marginal tax rate.
- **Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain:** Depreciation on real property (27.5-year assets) is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%.
- **Long-Term Capital Gain:** Gain above the total depreciation taken is taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).
- **Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT):** A 3.8% surtax may apply to investment income for taxpayers with modified adjusted gross income above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly).

The net effect of cost segregation is a deferral and partial conversion of tax liability. Depreciation deductions taken at ordinary income rates in the early years are partially recaptured at sale, but the time value of the tax deferral and the rate differential between ordinary income and capital gains rates typically produce a net benefit.

## IRA and Retirement Account Considerations

Investors considering using self-directed IRA or other retirement account funds should be aware of the following:

- **No Tax Benefit from Depreciation:** Since IRA and retirement accounts are already tax-deferred or tax-exempt, the depreciation and cost segregation benefits described above provide no additional tax savings.
- **UBTI/UBIT Risk:** Retirement accounts investing in leveraged real estate partnerships may be subject to Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI) on the portion of income attributable to debt-financed property. This can create a tax liability within the IRA.
- **Consult Your Tax Advisor:** The interaction between retirement accounts and real estate syndications is complex. Investors should consult with their CPA or tax advisor before investing retirement funds.

## Passive Activity Loss Rules

Depreciation losses from Parkview Commons are classified as passive losses under IRC Section 469. Key rules:

- Passive losses can offset **passive income** from other rental properties, partnerships, or syndications
- Passive losses **cannot** offset W-2 wages or active business income unless you qualify as a Real Estate Professional
- Unused passive losses **carry forward** indefinitely to offset future passive income
- All suspended passive losses are released upon disposition of your entire interest in the investment

Investors who qualify as a **Real Estate Professional** (750+ hours per year in real estate activities, exceeding time spent in all other trades or businesses) may be able to use these passive losses to offset active income. This determination is highly fact-specific and should be discussed with your CPA.
