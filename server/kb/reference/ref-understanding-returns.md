# Understanding Real Estate Investment Returns: IRR, Cash-on-Cash, and Equity Multiple

## Why Return Metrics Matter

Real estate syndications generate returns through **ongoing cash flow** (rental income after expenses) and **appreciation** (increased property value at sale). Different metrics capture different dimensions of performance, and no single metric tells the complete story.

## Cash-on-Cash Return (CoC)

### What It Measures
Cash-on-cash measures annual cash income as a percentage of your initial investment. It captures only actual cash distributions --- not paper gains, tax benefits, or unrealized appreciation.

**Formula:** `Annual Cash Distributions / Total Cash Invested`

**Example:** You invest $100,000 and receive $8,000 in annual distributions.
`$8,000 / $100,000 = 8.0% Cash-on-Cash Return`

### When It Matters Most
CoC is the best measure of current income yield. It answers: "What percentage of my capital am I receiving back as income each year?" Use it to compare syndications against bonds, dividend stocks, or savings accounts.

| Investment | Typical Annual Yield |
|------------|---------------------|
| High-yield savings | 4.0-5.0% |
| 10-year Treasury bond | 4.0-4.5% |
| S&P 500 dividend yield | 1.3-1.6% |
| Syndication (value-add) | 6-9% |

**Limitation:** CoC ignores appreciation and total return. A deal with low distributions but a large equity gain at exit could have low CoC but excellent total returns.

## Equity Multiple (EM)

### What It Measures
The equity multiple captures **total cash returned** over the investment's full life relative to total cash invested, including both distributions and sale proceeds.

**Formula:** `Total Cash Received / Total Cash Invested`

**Example:** You invest $100,000, receive $40,000 in cumulative distributions over 5 years, and receive $155,000 at sale.
`($40,000 + $155,000) / $100,000 = 1.95x`

You received $1.95 for every $1.00 invested --- a 95% total profit.

### When It Matters Most
EM is the simplest measure of total profitability. It answers: "How much total money will I get back per dollar invested?"

**Limitation:** EM ignores time. A 1.95x return over 3 years is far more attractive than 1.95x over 10 years.

## Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

### What It Measures
IRR is the annualized rate of return accounting for the **timing and magnitude** of every cash flow. It is the discount rate that makes the net present value of all cash flows equal to zero. Because IRR incorporates timing, receiving cash sooner increases the IRR.

### Worked Example
$100,000 invested, $8,000/year distributions for 5 years, $155,000 at sale in year 5:

| Year | Cash Flow |
|------|-----------|
| 0 | -$100,000 |
| 1 | +$8,000 |
| 2 | +$8,000 |
| 3 | +$8,000 |
| 4 | +$8,000 |
| 5 | +$163,000 |

**IRR: approximately 15.8%**

### Why Timing Matters

| Scenario | Hold Period | Equity Multiple | IRR |
|----------|------------|-----------------|-----|
| Deal A | 3 years | 1.95x | ~24% |
| Deal B | 7 years | 1.95x | ~10% |

Same total profit, but Deal A achieves it in less than half the time.

### When It Matters Most
IRR is the industry standard for comparing investments across different hold periods and distribution patterns.

**Limitation:** IRR can overstate short-hold deals and assumes distributions are reinvested at the same rate. Always evaluate IRR alongside the equity multiple.

## How the Three Metrics Work Together

| Metric | Best For | Key Limitation |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Cash-on-Cash** | Annual income yield | Ignores total return |
| **Equity Multiple** | Total profit magnitude | Ignores time |
| **IRR** | Time-adjusted annualized return | Can overstate short holds |

### Complete Example Summary

| Input | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Initial investment | $100,000 |
| Annual distributions | $8,000/yr (8% CoC) |
| Hold period | 5 years |
| Total distributions | $40,000 |
| Sale proceeds | $155,000 |
| **Total cash received** | **$195,000** |

| Return Metric | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Cash-on-Cash | 8.0% |
| Equity Multiple | 1.95x |
| IRR | 15.8% |

This tells a clear story: moderate current income (8%), strong total profitability (nearly doubling your money), achieved at an attractive annualized rate (15.8%) over a 5-year hold.
