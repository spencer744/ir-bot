# How to Read a Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)

## What Is a PPM and Why Does It Exist?

A Private Placement Memorandum is the primary legal disclosure document provided to prospective investors in a real estate syndication. It serves a function similar to a prospectus for a public security: disclosing all material information about the investment so you can make an informed decision. A well-drafted PPM does not sell the deal --- it discloses it, including everything that could go wrong.

PPMs are typically 80-200 pages of legal language. Understanding the key sections will help you distinguish strong opportunities from weak ones.

## Key Sections to Review

### Risk Factors
This is arguably the most important section. It enumerates every material risk: market downturns, interest rate changes, sponsor conflicts, environmental issues, regulatory uncertainties, and more. Read every risk factor, paying special attention to risks specific to this deal rather than generic industry boilerplate. A thorough risk disclosure signals a responsible sponsor.

### Use of Proceeds
This details exactly how investor capital is deployed: acquisition cost, closing costs, renovation reserves, operating reserves, financing costs, and sponsor fees. Compare what percentage of capital goes to fees versus actually working in the property.

**Red flag:** If upfront fees exceed 3-5% of total equity, ask the sponsor to justify each one. Be wary of vague line items like "other expenses."

### Fee Schedule
All GP compensation throughout the investment lifecycle:
- **Acquisition fee** (1-2% of purchase price) --- for sourcing and closing
- **Asset management fee** (1-2% of revenue or equity) --- ongoing oversight
- **Property management fee** (4-8% of revenue) --- may go to a GP-affiliated company
- **Construction management fee** (5-10% of renovation budget) --- capital improvement oversight
- **Disposition fee** (0.5-1% of sale price) --- paid at sale

Look for whether fees are within market norms and whether GP-affiliated entities create conflicts.

### Distribution Waterfall
Defines how cash and sale proceeds are divided between LPs and the GP. Key questions: Is the preferred return cumulative or simple? Is there a GP catch-up provision? How does the promote structure compare to similar deals?

**Red flag:** If you cannot clearly explain the waterfall after reading it twice, ask the sponsor for a plain-language summary. Complexity can obscure unfavorable terms.

### Exit Provisions
Describes when and how the sponsor plans to exit (sale, refinance, or both). Look for a defined hold period, flexibility to hold longer if conditions are unfavorable, and provisions for extension or early exit.

### Conflicts of Interest
Discloses situations where the GP's interests may diverge from LPs': affiliated management companies, multiple simultaneous deals, and opportunity allocation. Conflicts are not disqualifying if they are fully disclosed and reasonably managed. Does the GP co-invest meaningfully alongside LPs? Are affiliated fees at or below market rates?

### Subscription Requirements
Outlines minimum investment amounts, the subscription process, funding timeline, and accredited investor documentation requirements.

### Operating Agreement Summary
Key provisions include LP voting rights, transfer restrictions, GP removal provisions, reporting obligations, and capital call circumstances. Can LPs remove the GP for cause? What happens if you cannot fund a capital call?

## General Red Flags

- Projected returns significantly above market norms without clear justification
- Excessive or vaguely defined fees
- Inadequately disclosed conflicts of interest
- Overly optimistic language without supporting market data
- Sponsor with limited track record making aggressive projections
- High-leverage debt (above 75% LTV), short maturities, or unhedged variable rates
- Little or no GP co-investment alongside LPs
- No clear exit strategy or unrealistic exit cap rate assumptions

## Practical Advice

Read the PPM before committing capital. Take notes on anything unclear and ask the sponsor for clarification. Compare terms across multiple offerings to calibrate expectations. Consider having your attorney review the PPM for your first investment. A good sponsor welcomes thorough questions --- if a sponsor discourages careful review, that itself is a red flag.
