# The Midwest Multifamily Investment Thesis

## Why Geography Matters

In real estate, the market you invest in determines your purchase price, rental income potential, competitive landscape, and risk-adjusted returns. While coastal and Sun Belt markets dominate national headlines, the Midwest offers a compelling combination of fundamentals that produce strong, stable returns for multifamily investors.

## Lower Basis: More Property Per Dollar

The most important driver of the Midwest thesis is cost of entry:

| Market Type | Typical Cost Per Unit (Value-Add Class B) |
|------------|------------------------------------------|
| Coastal (NYC, LA, SF, Boston) | $250,000 - $400,000+ |
| Sun Belt (Austin, Nashville, Phoenix) | $200,000 - $350,000 |
| Midwest (Indianapolis, Columbus, Kansas City) | $80,000 - $130,000 |

Lower basis means less capital at risk, lower debt service, and a wider margin of safety. The same investment dollar buys more units and more diversified income.

## Higher Cash Yields

Lower purchase prices relative to rental income produce higher yields from day one. Midwest value-add multifamily routinely generates **7-9% cash-on-cash returns** versus **3-5%** in coastal markets. The math works because the ratio of rent to purchase price is far more favorable, even though absolute rents are lower.

**Example:** A 200-unit community in Indianapolis at $18M ($90K/unit) with $1,050 average rents generates a higher yield than the same quality asset at $60M ($300K/unit) with $2,200 rents in a coastal market, because the cost basis is proportionally much lower.

## Affordable Rents Drive Stability

In many Midwest markets, median apartment rents remain **below 25% of median household income** --- well within the 30% affordability threshold. This means tenants have income after rent, reducing turnover and delinquency. Affordable rents also insulate against demand destruction during slowdowns (tenants can still pay) and political risk (less pressure for rent control when housing is perceived as affordable).

Many coastal and high-growth markets have pushed rents above 30-40% of income, creating affordability stress and regulatory scrutiny that Midwest markets largely avoid.

## Diversified Employment Base

Major Midwest metros feature employment diversified across healthcare, logistics, technology, financial services, education, and advanced manufacturing. No single industry downturn dramatically impacts the overall employment picture.

Indianapolis exemplifies this: Eli Lilly has invested billions in local manufacturing expansion, IU Health is among the state's largest employers, Amazon and FedEx operate major distribution hubs, and Salesforce maintains a significant technology presence. This employer mix across sectors creates resilient housing demand.

## Limited New Supply

Construction starts in Midwest markets run **40-60% below** Sun Belt levels on a per-capita basis. New supply is the primary threat to rent growth --- when thousands of luxury units deliver simultaneously, existing properties face competitive pressure. Sun Belt markets that saw enormous construction booms in 2021-2023 are now grappling with elevated vacancy and rent compression.

In the Midwest, the combination of lower achievable rents and high construction costs relative to rents makes new development less economically attractive, creating a structural advantage for owners of existing properties.

## Less Institutional Competition

Coastal and Sun Belt markets attract heavy institutional capital --- pension funds, sovereign wealth, large PE firms --- that drives up pricing and compresses yields. Midwest deal sizes ($10-50M) sit below most institutional thresholds, creating a less competitive environment where disciplined operators acquire at more favorable pricing.

## The Indianapolis MSA

Indianapolis exemplifies the Midwest thesis across every dimension:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Metro population | ~2.15 million |
| Apartment vacancy rate | ~4.2% |
| Average annual rent growth | ~4.1% |
| Cost of living | ~10% below national average |

**Major employers:** Eli Lilly (pharmaceuticals), IU Health (healthcare system), Amazon (logistics), FedEx (distribution hub), Salesforce (technology), Rolls-Royce (aerospace), Elevance Health/Anthem (insurance). This mix of healthcare, logistics, life sciences, and technology provides long-term employment stability.

The metro sits at the crossroads of major interstates (I-65, I-69, I-70, I-74), making it a natural logistics hub. Population growth has been steady, driven by corporate relocations and employment corridor expansion rather than speculative migration.

## Risk-Adjusted Return Profile

Combining lower basis, higher yields, affordable rents, diversified employment, limited supply, and reduced competition, typical Midwest value-add targets:

| Metric | Typical Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Cash-on-Cash | 7-9% |
| Equity Multiple (5-year) | 1.8-2.1x |
| IRR | 14-18% |

The trade-off is more modest appreciation potential --- Midwest markets are unlikely to see dramatic value surges or crashes. For investors prioritizing consistent, income-driven returns with downside protection, this profile is compelling.
