# Accredited Investor Requirements and Verification

## Why Accredited Investor Status Exists

The SEC created the accredited investor standard to protect investors in private securities offerings. Because syndications are exempt from the full registration and disclosure requirements of publicly traded securities, the SEC limits participation to individuals and entities with sufficient financial resources or professional sophistication to evaluate and bear the risks of illiquid, non-registered investments. This is a federal legal requirement, not a sponsor policy.

## Qualification Criteria for Individuals

You qualify as an accredited investor if you meet **any one** of the following:

### Income Test
- **Individual:** Earned income exceeding **$200,000** in each of the two most recent years, with a reasonable expectation of the same in the current year
- **Joint with Spouse/Spousal Equivalent:** Combined income exceeding **$300,000** in each of the two most recent years, with a reasonable expectation of the same in the current year

### Net Worth Test
- **Individual or Joint:** Net worth exceeding **$1,000,000**, excluding the value of your primary residence

Your primary residence is excluded from the calculation entirely: do not count its value as an asset or its mortgage as a liability. Exception: if your mortgage exceeds the home's fair market value, the excess counts as a liability. HELOCs drawn within 60 days of investing count as liabilities.

Assets that count include cash, investment and retirement accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s), real estate other than your primary residence, and business interests.

### Professional Credentials
Holders of FINRA Series 7, Series 65, or Series 82 licenses qualify regardless of income or net worth.

## Entity Qualification

Entities can qualify through several paths: trusts, corporations, or LLCs with **assets exceeding $5 million** (not formed solely to invest); entities where **all equity owners** are individually accredited; banks, insurance companies, and registered investment companies; and **family offices** with $5M+ in assets under management.

Self-directed IRAs may invest if you, as the account holder, personally qualify as accredited.

## Verification Methods for 506(c) Offerings

**506(b) offerings** allow self-certification --- the investor represents their status in the subscription agreement, and the issuer must have a reasonable belief the investor qualifies.

**506(c) offerings** require the issuer to take **reasonable steps to verify** accredited status. Accepted methods include:

- **Income verification:** Review of tax returns (W-2s, 1099s, K-1s) for the prior two years plus a written representation for the current year; or a written letter from a licensed **CPA, attorney, registered investment adviser, or broker-dealer**
- **Net worth verification:** Review of bank/brokerage statements, tax assessments, and a credit report dated within 90 days; or a written letter from a licensed **CPA, attorney, registered investment adviser, or broker-dealer**
- **Third-party verification services:** Specialized services that review documentation and issue standardized verification letters, protecting investor privacy by limiting information shared with the sponsor

## Important Notes

Accreditation is verified **per investment** --- your status must be current at the time of each subscription. The SEC does not maintain a registry of accredited investors. Your unmarried partner may qualify as a spousal equivalent for both income and net worth tests if you share a household and financial life.
